Introduction¶

  • University of Calgary: a U15 research-intensive university, offering 50+ graduate programs, spanning a broad range of disciplines and approaches to research

  • Graduate theses produced by University of Calgary students represent the diversity of research produced at the University of Calgary

  • The Vault provides a valuable archive and dissemination vehicle for theses, but usage patterns and impact is not well understood

Guiding Questions¶

  1. Which disciplines are represented in the collection and what is the number of theses in each discipline?

  2. Is there any correlation between discipline and usage?

  3. Does usage change over time?

Data¶

  • Data for this project were provided by Libraries and Cultural Resources, University of Calgary.

  • Data were collected from PRISM: University of Calgary’s Digital Repository, October 6, 2018. Data from 4866 theses were included.

  • Two data files were collected, wrangled and merged:

    • Usage statistics data: Downloads for each thesis, December 2015 - September 2018 (34 months).

    • Thesis metadata: Descriptive metadata for 4866 theses.

    • Geographic data were not included due to the limitations of how geographic user information is collected in PRISM and time constraints for extracting this data into a format usable with our other two data sets.

Results¶

  • Used plotly and an interative design process to illustrate different aspects of this data

  • Some considerations we made in our visualizations:

    1. What are the best visual representations for this data?
    2. With 56 different disciplines represented and different ways to group within discipline, how much data can we pack into one visualization?
    3. What conclusions can an end user draw from each visualization?
  • Plotly met five of the seven categories of interaction as described by Yi, et al, 2007: Select, Explore, Reconfigure, Abstract, Filter

Visualization 1: Bar plot showing the number of theses per discipline¶

This visualization gives a sense of the volume of the electronic thesis collection and how many theses have been deposited from June 2012 - September 2018. We can observe from this chart that the largest volume of theses are from Chemical and Petroleum Engineering, Education and Biological Sciences. Plotly makes it easy to see exact numbers when hovering over a bar.

Visualization 5: Combination visualization with monthly downloads and new entries¶

This visualization combines the previous graph with a barchart, plotted in the negative y-axis to see as new theses came into the collection for each discipline. The purpose of this graph is to see if there is a correlation between upticks in downloads and new content. Initial observations seem to be that increase in downloads come into effect a month or two after new theses are added. Practically, this makes sense as new theses coming into the repository usually have a 1-2 month delay before they are open access.

Conclusion and Next Steps¶

1. Which disciplines are represented in the collection and what is the number of theses in each discipline?

56 disciplines represented in our thesis collection with wide variability in terms of number per discipline. Adding the thesis type dimension is interesting to visual to get a sense of the volume produced based on type.

2. Is there any correlation between discipline and usage?

Definitively yes. Certain disciplines were consistently high, others consistently low usage. The mean monthly download rate is a good way to normalize downloads based and make observations of proportional use.

3. Does usage change over time?

Yes, usage changes over time, but not in a predictable way. Upticks coincide with additions into the collection. Upticks observed outside of this pattern may be because of other external factors and need more study.

Next steps: Testing these visualizations with stakeholder groups to see what kinds of observations they can draw and assess usefulness. See what other visualizations would meet stakeholder needs for this data.
